The Paradox is founded on original qualitative research with executives and board members across sectors, interrogating what actually happens to decision quality when AI enters the room. The thesis was completed in May 2026. The findings are now being translated into practice through the governance platform, advisory engagements, and forthcoming publications.

The Thesis

The paradox of human–AI collaborative decision-making: dealing with a dual-bounded system.

Robert Timmer · Nyenrode Business University · May 2026

Sixteen semi-structured interviews with executives and board members across sectors in the Netherlands, Germany, and France. 1,080 coded quotations analysed through ATLAS.ti. Template analysis within an interpretivist epistemology.

Three interconnected outputs

01 / Output

Thirteen paradoxes

Empirical manifestations of the dual-bounded system — the interaction between human bounded rationality and AI’s bounded learning. Six document tensions in AI adoption and governance; seven document cognitive, emotional, and relational dynamics. Together they reveal a systematic knowing–doing gap: organisations understand the risks but lack the governance to address them.

02 / Output

Eight design principles

Clustered into four governance domains: governance and decision authority, strategic decision quality, organisational capability, and enabling conditions. Sequential in establishment, simultaneous in operation. These principles are operationalised in the boardroom governance platform.

03 / Output

Five novel dynamics

The commitment–confirmation cycle. The absent auto-corrective function. The noise amplification mechanism. The two-sided capability divide. Emotional outsourcing. Dynamics nobody else has named — and the intellectual foundation for everything The Paradox builds.

The core concept

A dual-bounded system.

Human bounded rationality and AI’s bounded learning do not cancel each other out. Where they overlap, their limits compound — and that intersection is where the thirteen paradoxes live. Cognitive offloading to AI can progressively degrade the very judgment it was meant to support: AI does not simply augment human decision-making, it transforms it — creating new challenges alongside new capabilities.
