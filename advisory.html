01 / Principle

Governance as design, not policy

Most governance frameworks treat AI as an input to be controlled. We treat it as a participant in the decision itself. The design question is not whether AI is permitted, but what happens to decision quality when it enters the room.

cognition

02 / Diagnosis

Dual bounded rationality

Humans and AI each operate under distinct cognitive constraints. Their interaction in decision-making does not cancel these limitations; it compounds them. This creates cascading dynamics that most governance does not yet see.

Why current frameworks fall short

AI is not an asset. It is a participant.

Most governance structures treat AI as an asset, like a software licence or a factory. That is a category error. AI is an emergent participant in the decision-making of the organisation.

The question is not whether the data was clean or the model approved, but whether the board is deciding better, or simply deciding with more confidence. Those are not the same thing.

The competitive advantage in AI is not capability. It is knowing where human and AI judgment both break down, and designing for it.

Every board is now a human–AI decision system. The ones that understand that will outgovern the ones that do not.
